Turing, biology and pattern formation

Gary Ballantyne and Martin Clark
[http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alan_Turing, Alan Turing] spent his last few years working on pattern formation in mathematical biology---making a [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Chemical_Basis_of_Morphogenesis, fundamental contribution] to [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reaction-diffusion_system, reaction-diffusion systems]. These systems describe an amazing array of natural phenomena in biology, chemistry, ecology and more. The Gray-Scott [http://people.maths.ox.ac.uk/trefethen/pdectb/reaction2.pdf,(pdf)] model, for instance, generates complex patterns from a relatively simple model [http://www3.nd.edu/~powers/pearson.pdf, (pdf)]: $$ \begin{align} U_t &= D_u \nabla^2 U - UV^2 + F(1-U) \nonumber \\ V_t &= D_v \nabla^2 V + UV^2 - (F+k)V \nonumber \end{align} $$ Here $U$ and $V$ are the spatial concentrations of two chemicals, which are particularly sensitive to parameters $F$ and $k$. The plot shows the evolution of $V$ from a randomized initial condition. Parameters $D_u=0.19$ and $D_v=0.05$ are fixed, but clicking the figure cycles through three combinations of $F$ and $k$.
The CoffeeScript is based on [http://www.loria.fr/~rougier/teaching/numpy/scripts/gray-scott.py, this Python].
